A Word-embedding model for drugs and adverse drug reactions to support the work of negative-effect detections

August 4, 2021

This is a project that I got involved at Uppsala Monitoring Centre (UMC - Link). Lucie G. has developed a word2vec (a natural language processing technique) model to build joint vector representations for drugs and adverse drug reactions (negative, side effects caused by drugs during treatment). Her work has been published (link) and used in signal (as known as potential side effects) detection at UMC.

My contribution to the project was that I applied Lucie’s model to carry out different experiments on the up-to-date WHO database of individual case safety reports. The experiments include:

1. Extract data from the WHO database to create a separate database for the model.

  • The new database significantly improves the model performance in term of less time consuming for pulling input data

2. Find out the best algorithm (Skip-Gram or CBOW) to train the word2vec model for drugs and side effects

  • Word2vec is a word embedding technique to process text by vectorising words. It can help to transform discrete-state words into vector space. It is widely used in parsing sentences, predicting word’s meaning, document clustering, sentiment analysis, etc.

  • Word2vec model is a shallow (two layers) neural network. It can be trained by two different methods sketched in the following graph: CBOW (A) and Skip-Gram (B). CBOW, stands for Continuous Bag of Words, trains the model by the process of calculating the conditional probability of generating a central word using given context words (surrounding words). In contrast, Skip-Gram trains the model by calculating the probability of generating context words using given central words.

A graph (adapted from the internet) showing CBOW (A) vs Skip-Gram (B)

  • In the work of monitoring post-marketing drugs to detect side (negative) effects - reactions, which is a branch of pharmacovigilance science, a traditional way is to look at individual case reports containing information about drugs and reactions. The first step is to perform statistical analysis to see if a combination of drug and reaction is statistically significant in a comparison with a certain background (comparator). If the combination is passed the first screening, it will be further investigated by experts in specific treatment fields. Drugs and reactions are discrete concepts they cannot fully represent the similarity between different medical concepts or the similarity of drugs. This is why the word2vec model becomes handy. The Word2vec model’s workflow

  • Rigorous tests with helps from pharmacovigilance scientists in manual assessment indicate that skip-gram is the best algorithm to train the model. It is in line with results from other text analysing applications using small corpora.

3. Hyperparameter tuning

  • The important hyper-parameters in model are window size (the number of words on both sides of each central word) and dimensionality. The grid search with a special metrics for evaluation pointed out a window size of 7 and dimensionality of 100.

4. Include drug indication in the training dataset

  • Including drug indication in the model’s corpora was performed in two different ways: (1) indications placed next to the corresponding drugs when constructing sentences, (2) concepts for drug, reactions, and indications were shuffled before constructing sentences regardless of positions of the concepts in the sentences. The results do not show any significant improvement compared to the model merely using drugs and reactions.

5. Model stability

  • Stability means that the model would reproduce a the same similarity (cosine of two vectors) for any pair of concepts if it is retrained, i.e., the model is trained for the first time to create vector space 1 and then retrained to create vector space 2, the two vector spaces are just different versions of one. I’ve used a metric called Procrustes in Scipy for similarity test between two vector spaces. The model has passed the test. It performs better for more common concepts compared to less common ones.

6. Group (cluster) all reactions reported with a given drug using vectors generated by the word2vec model.

  • We wanted to develop a new approach for the work of signal detections in pharmacovigilance. It will focus on identifying regions of interest in the vector space of reactions rather than focusing on individual concepts. If one reaction is found, a case series could be selected based on the distance in vector space to other reactions. I applied the K-means clustering method for the vectors representing the drugs and reactions. It can be done as the following steps:
    • Step 1. First, select a drug
    • Step 2. Get all reactions reported together with the drug
    • Step 3. Get vectors (from model’s outputs) for the reactions
    • Step 4. Select the total number of clusters (using K-Means + silhouette_score)
    • Step 5. Apply K-Means (again) to get reactions for each cluster
    • Step 6. For visualising the clusters, use PCA + TSNE.

The following plot shows five randomly selected clusters of reactions reported with with a given drugs (Drug X); hover over to the dots to see the names of the reactions. Interactive scatter plot showing reactions in five randomly selected clusters associated with Drug X.

Outlook:

The results from the word2vec model are used in the work of potential signals (adverse drug reactions) detection at UMC. The clustering method based on the word2vec model is needed further investigation, for example, if the clusters are clinically relevant or the case series and associated adverse event profiles can be identified by using the clusters.
