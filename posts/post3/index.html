Student's t-distribution and Confidence intervals using t-score

February 10, 2021

What is the T-distribution?

  • T-distribution is known as the Student distribution. T-distribution allows for use of small samples (n < 30) with an unknown standard deviation of population, however it does so by sacrificing certainty with a margin-of-error trade-off. It also has a bell-shaped distribution as normal distribution and it converges into a normal distribution when sample size n is high.

  • Probability density function: $$ f(t) = \frac {\varGamma(\frac{v+1}{2})}{\sqrt{v\pi}\varGamma(\frac{v}{2})} (1+\frac{t^2}{v})^{-\frac{v+1}{2}}$$ Where $$\varGamma$$ is the gamma function, and v = n-1 is the number of degrees of freedom.

  • t-score is analogous to the z-score (in normal distribution), since we don’t know the population standard deviation, so we need to use the sample standard deviation (SD): $$t=\frac{\overline{X}-\mu}{s/\sqrt{n}}$$ where $$\overline{X}$$ is the sample mean and s is the sample SD. t-distribution also has a t-table for t-score, i.e., knowing the degree of freedom and the desired cumulative probability.

  • t-test is commonly used for comparison between independent samples (while z-test is to compare a sample to the population).

Call T-distribution using Scipy?

#Import the stats library
from scipy.stats import t
# Import matplotlib
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

Confidence intervals using t-score

  • Confidence interval is a range that the measured parameters are expected to fall into with certain confidence (percentage). We can only have the sample’s parameter and you want to estimate a range for the corresponding parameter for the whole population. The lower and upper limits for the confidence intervals are:
    • UL = $\overline{X} $ + t-score * approx_stand_error
    • LL = $\overline{X} $ - t-score * approx_stand_error
  • We consider an example of scores in an exam for ten students and try to estimate the mean score for the school students using t-score with 98% confidence. The sample has the mean: $$\overline{X} = 120$$ and the sample standard deviation $$s = 29.51$$
from scipy.stats import t
X_bar = 126
n = 10
s = 29.51
# t-score for two-tailed
t_score =round(t.ppf(q=1-.02/2,df=n-1),2)
# Calculate upper and lower limits
UL = X_bar + t_score * s/(n)**(1/2)
LL = X_bar - t_score * s/(n)**(1/2)
print(f'With 98% confidence, the mean score for all the school students is in between: {LL} and {UL} ')

With 98% confidence, the mean score for all the school students is in between: 99.68409452205758 and 152.3159054779424

Hypothesis test

A study shows the average weight for schoolboys at ages of 14-15 years in an area is 150 lbs. To evaluate the study we take a sample of weights of 10 boys in the area. The sample mean is 155 lbs and a standard deviation of 12.6 lbs. We want to test the hypothesis (result from the study) with a significance level of 5%.

Step 1:

  • Define the hypotheses:

    • H_0: the population mean is equal 150, the average weight from study is correct.
    • H_a: the population mean is NOT equal 150, the average weight from study is NOT correct.

  • Significance level:

    • 5% Significance level (alpha = 0.025, two-tailed)
    • if there is less than 5% chance of occurring, then we’ll reject the null hypothesis, H_0.
from scipy.stats import t
# the sample mean, size, and standard deviation
X_b = 156
n = 10
s = 12.5
# the hypothesis mean
mu = 150

Step 2:

Identify test statistic: t-statistic $$t_s = \frac{\overline{X} - \mu}{\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}}$$

t_s = (X_b - mu) / ((s)/(n)**(1/2))
t_s

1.5178932768808222

Step 3:

Determine the p-value

p_value = 1-t.cdf(abs(t_s), df=n-1)
p_value

0.08167716372263945

Step 4:

Comparing p-value and the significance level (alpha = 0.02). Because the p_value is larger than alpha, we can NOT reject the null hypothesis.
